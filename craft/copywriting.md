## Universal AI-tells (Layer 1)

Hardcoded patterns that signal AI-generated prose regardless of tenant or form. Every Copywriter and the Social/Community Manager scans for these.

| # | Tell | What it looks like | Why it flags |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Throat-clearing openers** | "I want to talk about..." / "Let me share..." / "Today I'm going to tell you..." / "Here's a thought..." / "In this post we'll explore..." | Wastes the reader's most expensive attention beat |
| 2 | **Rhetorical-question openers** | "Ever wondered why..." / "What if I told you..." / "Have you ever found yourself..." | Performative; doesn't earn the open |
| 3 | **Restated-conclusion closes** | Final sentence paraphrases the opening sentence with different words | Symmetry that feels structured but lands flat |
| 4 | **Sentence-restatement** | Adjacent sentences expressing the same idea in different words | Filler dressed as emphasis |
| 5 | **Staccato fragments** | "Bold. Decisive. Built for you." / "Fast. Reliable. Simple." | Coded as punchy; reads as Mad-Lib |
| 6 | **Filler and hedge phrases** | "It's worth noting that..." / "It's important to understand..." / "Needless to say..." / "In many ways..." / "Generally speaking..." | Adds words without adding information |
| 7 | **Corporate platitudes** | See [Banned phrases](#banned-phrases-and-corporate-platitudes) | Content-free signalling |
| 8 | **Uniform sentence-length runs** | 5+ consecutive sentences of similar length | Robotic rhythm |
| 9 | **Em-dash overuse** | More than 1 em-dash per ~150 words | Pattern-matches as ChatGPT output |
| 10 | **Tricolons everywhere** | Triadic lists appearing more than once per ~300 words | Rhetorical device weaponised into a tic |
| 11 | **Comparative-and-contrastive overuse** | "Not X — Y." / "It's not about A, it's about B." appearing more than once per piece | Frame device overworked |
| 12 | **Emoji-as-bullet-point** | Bullet lists prefixed with rotating emoji (✅ ⚡ 🚀 ✨) more than twice in a row | LinkedIn-AI tell; flag especially in social |
| 13 | **Apologetic transitions** | "But here's the thing..." / "And here's where it gets interesting..." / "Now, you might be wondering..." | Reader-handholding that signals AI |
| 14 | **False precision** | "Studies show 73% of X..." without citation; rounded-but-specific stats invented to sound credible | Hallucination tell |
| 15 | **Conclusion-signalling phrases** | "In conclusion..." / "To wrap up..." / "The bottom line is..." / "At the end of the day..." | Spoken-speech filler in written form |

## Hook discipline

The hook is the first 1–3 lines. It does 80% of the work of getting the reader to read the next line. Most rewrite time goes here.

**Universal hook rules** — apply across every form:

- **Specific > clever.** "Watched a 4-person team save 11 hours/week with this one change" beats "Productivity hack."
- **Open *with* the thought, not *about* the thought.** No "I want to share a thought on X" — just open with the thought.
- **One idea per hook.** If the hook is doing two things, it's doing none.
- **No throat-clearing.** See AI-tell #1.
- **No rhetorical-question openers.** See AI-tell #2. Exception: a *genuine* question the reader has actually asked, repurposed verbatim — but that's rare.
- **Number-led hooks work** when the number is specific and earns its place. "47 teams" > "many teams." Avoid round numbers ("100% of customers" reads as marketing).
- **Trigger-named hooks work** when you can name a specific reader-side event ("Watching every B2B team kill its calendar twice in Q1...").
- **Mobile preview cutoff matters.** On most forms (email, social, search results) the first ~30–50 characters decide whether the reader keeps going. Front-load the strongest word.

**Hook angle taxonomy** — when generating multiple hook candidates, vary by angle, not by word choice:

| Angle | Pattern | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Curiosity** | Opens a loop, hints at unresolved tension | Cold audiences, opinion content |
| **Specificity** | Named number / named example / named time | B2B audiences who reward proof |
| **Benefit-led** | States the outcome upfront | Most-aware audiences ready to evaluate |
| **Contrarian / POV** | States a position that contradicts conventional wisdom | Thought-leadership, brand-building |
| **Story-fragment** | Drops the reader into a specific scene | Long-form, presentation, narrative social |
| **Direct / blunt** | Drops the polite framing | Premium / high-confidence brands |
| **Question (genuine)** | A real question the audience asks themselves | Some social, some email — sparingly |
| **Reframe** | Recasts a familiar idea in a new light | Opinion content, repurposing |
| **Trigger event** | Names a specific reader-side moment | Email, social opinion posts |
| **Urgency / scarcity** | Names a deadline, limit, or window | Launch, promotional |

**Rule for variant generation**: when producing 2+ hook candidates, each must be a *different angle*, not a different adjective. A one-word swap is not a variant.

## Sentence rhythm

Good prose varies sentence length deliberately. Robotic prose marches in lockstep.

**Diagnostic — count consecutive sentences of similar length**:

- 5+ consecutive sentences within ±3 words of each other → flag (uniform rhythm)
- 5+ consecutive sentences of <8 words → flag (staccato run)
- 5+ consecutive sentences of >25 words → flag (compound-clause swamp)

**Repair**: introduce one sentence of dramatically different length (short among long, or long among short). The break in rhythm restores the reader's attention.

**Read aloud as the diagnostic.** Anywhere your breath runs out, the sentence is too long. Anywhere you trip on the rhythm, the sentences are too uniform.

## Restatement detection

Two adjacent sentences expressing the same idea in different words. The second sentence adds no information; it just repackages.

**Examples** (each pair is one restatement):

- "The dashboard was misleading. The data wasn't telling the truth."
- "Founders need to understand their customers deeply. A genuine understanding of the audience is essential for any founder."
- "We're rebuilding the product. The platform is getting a complete rewrite."

**Repair**: delete one of the two sentences. If both feel necessary, the second one is doing something different — find what, and write *that*.

**Strictness scaling**: see [Strictness by form](#strictness-by-form-the-scaling-table). Long-form tolerates 1 restatement per piece (rhetorically deliberate); short-form / email / social / presentation tolerate zero.

## Banned phrases and corporate platitudes

These signal corporate boilerplate or AI-generated prose. Default: zero allowed. Tenant override possible if the brand uses one deliberately as positioning.

**Tier 1 — high-confidence flag** (banned everywhere unless tenant explicitly allows):

- leverage (as a verb)
- synergy / synergies
- ecosystem (unless literal — a software ecosystem with real APIs counts; a "customer ecosystem" doesn't)
- robust (in marketing context; engineering context is fine)
- pivotal
- holistic
- unlock (in marketing context — "unlock potential / value / growth")
- foster (in marketing context — "foster engagement / community")
- curate / curated (overused; only meaningful when there's genuine selection)
- supercharge
- game-changer / game-changing
- next-level
- world-class
- best-in-class
- cutting-edge
- state-of-the-art
- mission-critical
- end-to-end (as marketing claim; fine as literal description of scope)
- seamless / seamlessly
- frictionless (overused)
- empower / empowering
- elevate (as verb in marketing)
- transform / transformative (overused; allow if a real transformation is being described)
- revolutionize / revolutionary
- innovative / innovation (allowed sparingly; flag if used more than once per piece)
- solutions (as a noun for "what we sell")
- delight / delightful (overused)
- journey (as customer-journey marketing-speak; allowed if literal)

**Tier 2 — context-dependent** (flag if used, allow with reasoning):

- bespoke (often pretentious; OK if genuinely custom work)
- impactful (often vague; OK if the impact is named)
- meaningful (often vague; OK if the meaning is named)
- powerful (often empty; OK if the power is specified)
- intuitive (overclaim; needs proof)
- thoughtful / thoughtfully (overused signal)

**Strictness scaling**: every form is zero-tolerance on Tier 1 unless tenant overrides. Tier 2 = scrutiny, not automatic ban.

## Opener and closer patterns

**Banned openers** (every form):

| Opener pattern | Why it flags |
|---|---|
| "I'm excited to announce..." | LinkedIn-AI tell #1 |
| "I'm thrilled to share..." | Same |
| "It is with great pleasure that..." | Corporate-press-release tell |
| "Hi [Name], I hope this email finds you well." | Email-AI tell |
| "I wanted to reach out because..." | Email-AI tell |
| "Just checking in..." | Email-AI tell |
| "In today's fast-paced world..." | Generic AI essay opener |
| "In an era of [X]..." | Same |
| "Let me start by saying..." | Throat-clearing |
| "First, let me share..." | Throat-clearing |
| "Ever wondered why..." | Rhetorical-question opener |
| "Have you ever..." | Rhetorical-question opener |

**Banned closers** (every form):

| Closer pattern | Why it flags |
|---|---|
| "In conclusion..." | Spoken-form filler in writing |
| "To wrap up..." | Same |
| "At the end of the day..." | Cliché |
| "The bottom line is..." | Cliché |
| Restating the opening sentence with different words | Restated-conclusion close (AI-tell #3) |
| "Hope this helps!" (in email body or social) | Throwaway close |
| "Let me know your thoughts!" (without a specific prompt) | Generic engagement-bait |

**Repair pattern**: rewrite the close to be a *new* line — a specific image, a forward-leaning question, a concrete next step, or a deliberate landing on the strongest single sentence the piece contains.

## The "earns the next sentence" principle

Every sentence's job is to earn the reader's commitment to the next sentence. If a sentence doesn't earn the next one, cut it.

**Diagnostic**: read the piece sentence-by-sentence. After each one, ask "would I keep reading?" If you wouldn't, the sentence has failed.

**Most common failure mode**: the second sentence. The hook earns the second sentence; the second sentence has to earn the third. Most AI prose loses the reader at sentence 2 because the writer relaxed.

---

## The one-thing principle

Every piece of prose is about ONE thing. If you can't state the piece's single point in one sentence shorter than the piece itself, the piece is doing too much.

**Diagnostic**: write the one-sentence summary first. If the piece wanders from it, cut. If the summary feels thin, the piece doesn't have enough to say.

**Form-specific application**:

- **Email**: one idea per email. Sequences distribute multiple ideas across emails.
- **Social post**: one idea per post. Threads distribute multiple ideas across posts.
- **Long-form article**: one POV per piece. Sub-arguments support the central POV.
- **Landing page**: one decision the reader is being asked to make. Everything on the page supports that decision.
- **Presentation**: one big idea per talk. Key messages (3–5) support the big idea.
- **Tagline**: one positioning thought, compressed.

| Filler/hedge phrases (#6) | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| Tier 1 banned phrases (#7) | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| Uniform sentence-length runs (#8) | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| Em-dash overuse (#9) | <1 per 150 words | <1 per 150 words | <1 per 150 words | 0 per piece | <1 per 200 words | 0 per piece |
| Tricolons (#10) | <1 per 300 words | <1 per 300 words | 0 | 0 | <1 per 500 words | 0 |
| Comparative-contrastive overuse (#11) | 1 per piece | 1 per page | 0 | 0 | 1 per talk | 0 |
| Emoji-bullet (#12) | n/a | n/a | n/a | n/a | n/a | <2 consecutive |
| Apologetic transitions (#13) | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| False precision (#14) | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| Conclusion-signalling phrases (#15) | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 | 0 |

**Overall principle**: the shorter the form, the stricter the strictness. Brevity makes drift disproportionately visible. Long-form has room for one deliberate punchy pair; short-form has zero room.

## Variant discipline

A variant must test a *different angle*, not a different *word choice*.

**Real variants** — test different angles, openings, structures:

- Hook A is curiosity-led ("The mistake I should have seen coming")
- Hook B is specificity-led ("47 founders, one pattern")
- Hook C is contrarian-POV ("Stop running customer interviews")

**Fake variants** — same angle, swapped words:

- A: "The mistake I should have seen coming"
- B: "The mistake I didn't see coming"
- C: "The error I should have noticed sooner"

Fake variants give the operator no testable hypothesis. Real variants give the operator a learning even when one loses.

**When variants are mandatory** (per form):

- **Email** — sequence-key emails (first email + main conversion email): full A/B with different subject + different opener + different angle
- **Short-form** — paid ad copy: 2+ variants per ad set, each a different angle
- **Social** — launch posts + paid amplification posts: hook A/B minimum
- **Web** — landing-page hero: optional A/B; if A/B'd, different hook + different subhead + different primary-CTA framing
- **Long-form** — typically single canonical version; variants only for high-leverage articles tested in distribution (different teasers / promotion lines)
- **Presentation** — variants are for delivery context (full vs lightning vs keynote), not for content A/B

## Layer 3 overlays by form

Each form has form-specific scan rules that run alongside Layer 1 (this file) + Layer 2 (tenant voice).

- **Email**: deliverability scan — spam triggers, image:text ratio, missing unsubscribe / footer. See [sub-edit-pipeline.md#layer-3-email](sub-edit-pipeline.md#layer-3-email).
- **Presentation**: spoken-friendliness — tongue-twisters, acronyms-spoken-aloud, spoken numbers. See [sub-edit-pipeline.md#layer-3-presentation](sub-edit-pipeline.md#layer-3-presentation).
- **Social**: platform-native scan — char limits, hook truncation, hashtag count/format, accessibility (alt text + captions), disclosure mandatories, platform-specific CTA shape. See [platform-native-social.md](platform-native-social.md) and [sub-edit-pipeline.md#layer-3-social](sub-edit-pipeline.md#layer-3-social).
- **Web**: AEO/SEO discipline — primary keyword placement, well-formed heading hierarchy, answerable subheadings, schema-readiness. Lives in `craft/web-aeo.md` (future build).
- **Long-form**: light AEO only if destination is HTML / owned blog / Medium-clone.
- **Short-form**: SEO discipline for paid-search ad copy only (keyword in headline).
